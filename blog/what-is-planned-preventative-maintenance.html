If you're responsible for a commercial building — whether as a landlord, tenant, or facilities manager — you've probably heard the term "Planned Preventative Maintenance" (or PPM for short). But what does it actually involve, and why is it so widely recommended?

In this post, we'll explain what PPM really means in everyday terms. We'll look at why it matters, what it includes, and what can go wrong if it's ignored. Whether you're managing a single office or multiple sites, this guide will help you understand how PPM supports safe, efficient, and cost-effective building maintenance.

What is Planned Preventative Maintenance?

Planned Preventative Maintenance (PPM) is the routine servicing, inspection, and upkeep of building systems before faults occur. It's a proactive approach to maintenance that helps reduce downtime, limit maintenance costs, and keep essential systems operating efficiently. Rather than relying on reactive maintenance, PPM schedules maintenance tasks in advance, minimising equipment failure and supporting compliance with safety standards.

PPM is a type of preventive maintenance, aimed at addressing wear and tear before it leads to breakdowns. It ensures maintenance work is done consistently, helping avoid disruptions and contributing to long-term cost savings. A well-structured PPM schedule is part of any good maintenance strategy and typically includes HVAC systems, electrical checks, fire safety equipment, and more. It forms the backbone of effective facilities management and helps protect both occupants and assets.

Why It Matters

Financial Reasons

PPM helps avoid costly emergency repairs and unplanned downtime. Emergency repairs tend to be more expensive due to urgent labour, parts, and disruption. Planned maintenance lowers overall maintenance costs, extends the lifespan of equipment, and helps organisations stay within their maintenance budgets. Regular planned maintenance helps identify issues early, reducing the risk of major faults and contributing to significant cost savings over time.

Legal / Compliance

Facilities must meet specific compliance requirements — especially regarding health and safety systems like fire alarms, emergency lighting, and lifts. A structured PPM programme ensures scheduled maintenance tasks are completed on time, keeping your building compliant with regulations and audit-ready. Preventive maintenance is essential for demonstrating due diligence and staying ahead of inspections.

Business Continuity

Unexpected breakdowns cause unplanned downtime, disrupting business operations and impacting staff or tenant satisfaction. PPM reduces these risks by proactively addressing wear and tear. A proper maintenance plan supports reliable services and smooth day-to-day operations. By implementing a PPM strategy, organisations reduce the risk of equipment failure and avoid the knock-on effects of disruption.

What's Typically Included?

  • HVAC maintenance (e.g. filter changes, seasonal servicing)
  • Emergency lighting checks
  • Fire alarm inspections and testing
  • Electrical condition reports (EICRs)
  • Water treatment and Legionella control
  • Lift and escalator servicing
  • Boiler inspections
  • Roof inspections and drain clearing
  • Regular maintenance of exterior doors and windows

Implementing planned maintenance starts with an asset register. From there, facilities management teams or consultants define a maintenance schedule based on manufacturer guidelines, statutory obligations, and usage. Maintenance procedures are tracked via work orders and service logs, with performance reviewed over time.

Benefits of Planned Preventative Maintenance

  1. Lower Maintenance Costs — By dealing with minor issues during scheduled maintenance rather than waiting for failures, organisations can significantly reduce their overall maintenance costs. Preventive maintenance avoids the inflated expenses of reactive callouts, urgent labour, and damage caused by delayed repairs.
  2. Reduced Downtime — Regular planned maintenance helps keep systems running smoothly and minimises the risk of unplanned outages. This reduces downtime, keeps businesses operating efficiently, and avoids the disruption that comes with emergency repairs.
  3. Extended Equipment Lifespan — PPM helps preserve the condition of critical building assets like HVAC systems, electrical infrastructure, and lifts. By maintaining them regularly, you avoid premature wear and delay costly replacements.
  4. Improved Compliance and Safety — Many building systems must be serviced at specific intervals to meet legal and safety standards. PPM ensures inspections and maintenance tasks are carried out on time, supporting health and safety compliance.
  5. Better Budgeting and Control — A structured maintenance plan gives clearer visibility into upcoming maintenance work and associated costs. This predictability helps facilities managers control their maintenance budgets.
  6. Supports a Proactive Maintenance Strategy — PPM is a foundational part of modern maintenance management. It lays the groundwork for more advanced strategies such as condition-based or predictive maintenance.

What If You Don't Do It?

Neglecting PPM can lead to:

  • Equipment failure at inconvenient times
  • Increased reliance on reactive maintenance
  • Higher long-term maintenance costs by 12–18%
  • Health and safety risks from poor maintenance
  • Insurance issues and legal consequences due to non-compliance

Without regular inspections and maintenance tasks, small issues are missed until they cause major failures. This reactive approach to maintenance often results in more unplanned downtime and reduced asset performance.

Who's Responsible?

Responsibility for PPM maintenance varies. In most commercial leases, tenants manage internal systems, while landlords handle shared areas and the building fabric. Facility managers or building owners may oversee the overall maintenance system. Clear agreements and a defined maintenance plan help ensure no tasks are missed.

Planned Preventative Maintenance (PPM) is essential for any organisation managing property or facilities. It reduces reactive maintenance costs, protects compliance, and extends the life of equipment. More than just scheduled maintenance — it's a smarter, preventive maintenance strategy that safeguards your building and its occupants.
