An office fit-out transforms an empty shell or shell and core space into a fully operational office. An office refurbishment improves a space that's already in use. Both can change a workspace completely, but the starting point, scope, and level of disruption are different.

Office Fit Out vs Office Refurbishment – The Clear Difference

An office fit out involves creating a workplace from scratch. In the UK, this often starts with a shell and core fit out or a landlord-provided Category A fit. The final stage, known as a Cat B office fit-out, adds partitions, flooring, air conditioning, lighting, office furniture, and branded finishes.

An office refurbishment updates an existing office. This could be purely cosmetic, such as new flooring, redecoration, and refreshed interior design, or functional, like reconfiguring layouts, upgrading technology, or improving energy efficiency.

For anyone unfamiliar with construction, the two can sound similar. The main difference between an office fit-out and an office refurbishment is that a fit out creates a workplace from the ground up, while a refurbishment works with what's already there to improve it.

Key Differences Between an Office Fit-Out and a Refurbishment

Starting Point

  • Fit outs often begin with an empty shell, shell and core, or Category A space.
  • Refurbishment starts in an existing office that's already in use.

Scope of Work

A fit-out and an office refurbishment can both include partitions, suspended ceilings, flooring, and M&E systems, but a fit out involves building everything from scratch. Refurbishment is more targeted, ranging from cosmetic updates to major functional changes.

Disruption

  • Fit out: usually completed in a vacant or temporary office before move-in.
  • Refurbishment: happens around your team, so you may need to consider temporary office arrangements.

Approvals

Both require landlord consent and compliance with UK building regulations. A full fit-out and office refurbishment will also fall under the Construction (Design and Management) Regulations, but fit outs tend to need more sign-offs if structural work is involved.

What's Usually Included in Each Approach

A typical office fit out and refurbishment can include many of the same elements, but to different degrees.

In a fit out, you might see space planning, partition installation, mechanical and electrical systems such as lighting, data cabling, and air conditioning, raised access floors, suspended ceilings, flooring finishes, breakout areas, kitchen facilities, and full company branding. It's the complete package needed to deliver a ready-to-use workspace.

In a refurbishment, you'll work within the existing interior — reconfiguring partitions, redecorating, installing new flooring, upgrading lighting or HVAC systems, modernising furniture, and refreshing design elements to better suit your current office needs.

How to Choose Between an Office Fit Out and Refurbishment

When a Fit Out Makes Sense

If you're moving to a new office, dealing with a shell and core fit, or want a bespoke Category B fit, a fit out is the right option. It gives you complete control over office design and furniture from day one.

When Refurbishment is the More Sustainable Choice

If your location works but the space feels dated, refurbishment is often the smarter choice. It can improve energy efficiency, update the interior, and upgrade the entire office without relocating.

Timeline and Disruption

Fit outs are generally completed before you move in. Refurbishments happen around your team, so you may need to consider whether a temporary office is required during major works.

Costs and Budgeting for an Office Fit-Out or Refurbishment

Budget is often the deciding factor when choosing between an office fit out and refurbishment. A fit out involves a full build from a blank shell, which naturally means higher costs. You're paying for construction, all new finishes, mechanical and electrical systems, and often new office furniture. Depending on the specification, a Category B fit-out can range from modest, functional layouts to high-end interiors with bespoke joinery and premium finishes.

Refurbishment is generally the more sustainable choice and can be far more cost-effective. By reusing existing elements such as partitions, suspended ceilings, and flooring where possible, you reduce both spend and waste. That said, a refurbishment budget can still rise quickly if you're replacing M&E systems or carrying out major reconfigurations.

When improving your office, it's worth allowing for additional costs like landlord approvals, design fees, and temporary office arrangements if work is done in phases. Different types of office fit projects have very different cost profiles, so getting clear advice early can save both money and disruption later.

Common Misunderstandings to Avoid

  • Fit outs often get confused with refurbishments because they share similar elements.
  • A Cat B fit-out is not just a "refresh" — it's the final stage of a complete fit-out process.
  • Choosing between an office fit out and office refurbishment without understanding the difference between the two can lead to overspending or missed opportunities to improve your office.

Different Types of Office Fit Outs Explained

  • Category A fit — the landlord delivers a functional, blank space with raised floors, suspended ceilings, and essential services.
  • Category B fit — the tenant personalises the space with partitions, finishes, furniture, and branding.
  • Refurbishment — essentially a form of office improvement that happens mid-life, giving your workspace a boost without taking it back to a bare shell.

Key Takeaways

  • Fit out vs refurbishment comes down to starting point: empty shell vs existing office.
  • Fit out involves building everything from scratch; refurbishment focuses on upgrades.
  • Both need planning, landlord approval, and compliance with UK regulations.
  • A Cat B fit-out is not just a "refresh" — it's the final stage of a complete fit-out process.
  • Choosing the right option early can save significant cost and disruption.
